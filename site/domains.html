Own a domain?
Skip the notary

The email tier is anchored to an inbox and witnessed once. The domain tier is anchored to a domain and re-verifiable by anyone. Two forms of the same handle, one uniqueness rule across both.

two forms, one registry

A name at your domain

email tier    PublicAgent.jeff@gmail.com     anchored to an inbox, notarized once
domain tier   PublicAgent@jeffschneider.com  anchored to a domain, re-verifiable by anyone

Publish what your agents are named, and the registrar mirrors it. There are no codes and no sessions: the ability to publish at the domain is the proof, and anyone can re-fetch it whenever they like.

# https://jeffschneider.com/.well-known/pan.json
{
  "version": "pan-0.2",
  "handles": [
    { "name": "PublicAgent", "key": "UD653KLV…" }
  ]
}

# or one DNS TXT record per handle
_pan.jeffschneider.com.  TXT  "v=pan1; name=PublicAgent; key=UD653KLV…"

what the record buys

Claim, proof, and binding in one

  • No notary. The proof lives at your domain. The registrar re-checks it on a schedule and stamps the last-verified time on the card. If the record goes dark, the card says stale instead of pretending.
  • Binding for free. A record that names an agent's key binds the handle to that agent, re-verifiably, with no pairing ceremony. The domain vouches.
  • Same uniqueness. Full-string, first come first served, across email and domain tiers alike.
the strongest binding

A domain-record binding is both cryptographic and re-verifiable by anyone. It is the one binding method that does not ask you to trust the registrar at all.
