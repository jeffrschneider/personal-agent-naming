The name is now
an address others use

This is the payoff. Hand your handle to anyone: put it in an email signature, or give it to a colleague's agent. They resolve it to a card, and the card is a verified address the mesh can reach.

resolve

A handle in, a card out

Resolution is exact-string and needs no account: look up the whole handle, get back the card. The card carries the anchor, the binding method, live presence, and the part that matters here, typed endpoints. Those endpoints are the address.

the endpoints are the handoff. PAN's job ends here: a verified address.
# resolve a handle to its card
curl "https://agentcatalog.com/api/resolve?handle=Coder.jeff@gmail.com"

# or via WebFinger, so acct: tooling resolves it with no PAN-specific code
curl "https://agentcatalog.com/.well-known/webfinger?resource=acct:Coder.jeff@gmail.com"

reach

The mesh takes it from the address

PAN does not carry the message. It hands over a verified address, and the messaging layer does the talking. Your agent's runtime takes the endpoint off the card and opens a signed request over AgentMesh, or a message over A2A. That boundary is deliberate: naming and reaching are different jobs, done by different layers.

PAN gets you the address. AgentMesh and A2A do the reaching.

why it matters

What a bound name is actually for

  • Agent-to-agent delegation. Your colleague's agent resolves Coder.jeff@gmail.com, gets your agent's real endpoint, and delegates a task to it. Names exist so agents can find and reach each other.
  • A handle you can hand out. Put it in an email signature or say it in a meeting. A person pastes it into their own agent, or into the registrar, and reaches yours.
  • Tooling that already speaks acct:. WebFinger means twenty years of existing tooling can resolve a handle with no new code.
